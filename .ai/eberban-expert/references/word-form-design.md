# Word Form Design — Sound Symbolism & Sizing

## Word Weight Strategy

Think in **syllables**, not character count. `CVCV` (4 chars) and `CCVCV` (5 chars) have roughly the same weight — both are ~2 syllables. What matters is how heavy the word feels when spoken, not how many letters it has.

- **1 syllable (3 chars)** — reserved for core high-frequency utility words (language-level important concepts). CCV = transitive equiv, CVC = intransitive. Scarce and precious — only for structural/utility words that appear in a huge variety of sentences, never for domain vocabulary.
- **~2 syllables (4-5 chars)** — common everyday words. This is the bulk of vocabulary. `CVCV`, `CCVV`, `CVCCV`, `CCVCV` all feel similar in weight. Pick whichever shape sounds best for the concept.
- **~3 syllables (5-6 chars)** — less frequent or more specialized concepts. Longer shapes like `CVCCVV`, `CCVCCV` etc.

## Sound Symbolism

Eberban word forms are not random — they should "feel right" for their meaning. Discord community members traditionally choose forms based on how they sound and whether they resonate with the concept. This is about the concept itself, not about matching sounds from any specific natural language.

**These are gentle guidelines, not rigid rules.** Use them as inspiration when crafting or selecting word forms. Sometimes a randomly generated word just sounds perfect — trust that instinct over the tables below. The goal is to make vocabulary feel alive, not to mechanically apply a formula.

### Consonant-Meaning Associations

| Sound class | Consonants | Evokes | Good for |
|------------|-----------|--------|----------|
| Nasals | m, n | soft, warm, cute, gentle, intimate | small animals (cat), food (milk, honey), affection |
| Plosives (voiceless) | p, t, k | sharp, crisp, sudden, precise | impact (hit, push), small hard things (stone, salt) |
| Plosives (voiced) | b, d, g | heavy, solid, forceful, deep | large animals (bear, cow), strong emotions (anger, guilt) |
| Fricatives (voiceless) | f, s, c | airy, flowing, soft continuous | fog, snow, calm, gentle movement (swim, float) |
| Fricatives (voiced) | v, z, j | buzzing, energetic, vibrant | bee, excited, storm |
| Liquids | l, r | smooth, flowing, alive, liquid | river, leaf, milk, run |

### Vowel-Meaning Associations

| Vowel | Evokes | Good for |
|-------|--------|----------|
| i | small, thin, sharp, bright, high | ant, bee, knife, lemon, insect |
| e | medium, clear, precise, neutral | general purpose, moderate concepts |
| a | open, wide, big, bold, strong | mountain, bear, anger, large things |
| o | round, soft, warm, enclosed | apple, egg, honey, sweet things |
| u | deep, dark, heavy, closed, serious | guilt, fear, deep water, night |

### Onomatopoeia

When a concept has a strong universal sound association, lean into it:
- Cat: m- sounds (meow/miaou across languages)
- Bee: bz/z- sounds (buzz)
- Frog: kr/gr- sounds (croak)
- Snake: s- sounds (hiss)
- Hit/strike: sharp plosive (t, k, p)
- Flow/water: l, r sounds

### Texture & Feel

- **Fluffy/soft things**: soft consonants (m, n, l) + round vowels (o, u)
- **Sharp/dangerous things**: hard consonants (t, k, p) + short/sharp vowels (i, e)
- **Heavy things**: voiced stops (b, d, g) + low vowels (a, o, u)
- **Light things**: voiceless consonants (f, s, p) + high vowels (i, e)
- **Smooth things**: liquids (l, r) + flowing vowels
- **Rough/harsh things**: fricatives (z, j) + plosives (k, g)

## Examples from Existing Vocabulary

- `mian` — cat. m- is soft/cute, -ian has a gentle nasal ending. Evokes "miaou."
- `meon` — apple. m- soft, -eon round and sweet sounding.
- `baluor` — stone/rock. b- heavy, -al- solid, -uor deep and grounded.
- `gzeon` — river. gz- energetic onset, -eon flowing.
- `tcuin` — tree. tc- firm, -uin grounded and stable.

## Process

1. Consider the concept: what does it feel like, sound like, evoke?
2. Pick consonants and vowels that resonate with that feeling.
3. Match length to frequency: 4 chars for everyday, 5-6 for less common.
4. Match transitivity to place structure: vowel-final = transitive, consonant-final = intransitive.
5. Validate against morphology rules (SSM compliance, valid consonant pairs).
6. Check for collisions with existing dictionary entries.
7. Say it out loud — does it feel right for the concept?
